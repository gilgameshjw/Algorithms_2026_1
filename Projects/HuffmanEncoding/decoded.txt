Methodology of Computer Science: The Integration of Natural Science and Technical Methods
1 Methodological Foundation of Computer Science
Computer science, as a discipline that integrates the rigor of natural sciences with the practicality of engineering technology, incorporates a methodology that combines multiple paradigms of formal reasoning, experimental verification, and engineering practice. This methodological system enables computer science to explore the essence of computational theory while constructing reliable systems to solve practical problems. Compared to other natural sciences and engineering technology fields, computer science's methodology uniquely integrates the precision of mathematical logic, the empiricism of physical experiments, and the systematic nature of engineering design, forming a hierarchical and mutually supportive research system.
The methodology of computer science comprises three core research paradigms: theory, abstraction, and design. These three paradigms correspond to the typical problem-solving processes within the discipline. The theoretical paradigm employs mathematical methods, using definitions, axioms, theorems, and proofs to establish and understand the mathematical principles underlying computer science; its research possesses distinct characteristics of constructive mathematics. The abstraction paradigm originates from experimental science, exploring potential algorithms, data structures, and system architectures through data collection, formal specification of hypotheses, model construction and prediction, and experimental analysis. The design paradigm stems from engineering, constructing systems and devices to solve given problems through requirements specification, design, and implementation methods.

2 Relationship and Differences Between Computer Science and Natural Science Methodology
Computer science shares fundamental methodological principles with the natural sciences, particularly in its commitment to hypothesis testing, model construction, and empirical validation. Like natural scientists conducting controlled experiments, computer scientists employ rigorous techniques such as benchmarking and systematic performance analysis to evaluate computational systems. The discipline relies heavily on modeling—constructing abstract representations of algorithms, data structures, and system architectures—which enables researchers to formulate predictions, test theoretical assumptions, and analyze computational boundaries through experimental methods.
What distinguishes computer science is its unique focus on artificial rather than natural systems. This artificial nature integrates the exploratory approach of science with the constructive paradigm of engineering, creating a discipline concerned with both understanding existing computational phenomena and designing new computational possibilities. This dual character manifests in a continuous cycle where theoretical principles guide abstract modeling, these models inspire practical designs, and design experiences subsequently refine and enrich theoretical understanding—a process vividly illustrated in how hash table evolution combines theoretical analysis, probabilistic modeling, and engineering implementation.
3 Core Research Methods in Computer Science
Computer science employs several core research methods that form its methodological foundation. Theoretical research builds upon mathematical logic, utilizing formal proofs, complexity analysis, and axiomatic systems to establish computing's rigorous foundations. Through discrete mathematics and graph theory, researchers examine computational processes' essential properties and limits, while formal methods like model checking and theorem proving provide mathematical verification for critical systems. This theoretical framework enables computer scientists to analyze fundamental constraints and possibilities in computation, as demonstrated in distributed algorithms research where formal models address coordination problems in distributed environments.
The experimental paradigm in computer science follows systematic procedures to validate computational hypotheses and evaluate system performance. Researchers employ controlled experiments, case studies, and benchmark testing following the "hypothesis-verification-analysis" model, carefully defining independent and dependent variables to ensure valid results. From large language model evaluation to computer vision research, experimental methods provide empirical evidence through quantitative metrics and comparative analysis, with proper benchmarking ensuring reproducible and comparable results across the research community.
Engineering and design methods bring a practical dimension to computer science, focusing on systematic approaches to developing reliable software systems. These methods encompass requirements analysis, specification, implementation, and testing, manifested through various development methodologies including the sequential waterfall model, iterative spiral model with risk analysis, and flexible agile approaches. The design science paradigm further contributes knowledge through artifact construction, following a cyclical process of problem identification, solution design, implementation, and evaluation that connects theoretical insights with practical applications in areas like hash table design and distributed algorithm implementation.
Computational methods and programming paradigms constitute the executive core of computer science, providing practical tools for solving computational problems. Multiple programming paradigms—including procedural, object-oriented, and functional programming—offer different approaches to code organization and system design, while concurrent and parallel computing address the challenges of simultaneous process execution. Algorithmic techniques such as divide-and-conquer, dynamic programming, and approximation algorithms provide reusable templates for problem-solving, with the concept of trade-offs remaining central to algorithm design as practitioners balance competing concerns like time versus space efficiency and generality versus specialization.

4 Development and Challenges of Modern Computer Science Research Methods
Modern computer science research is increasingly characterized by AI-driven methodologies, where machine learning serves not only as a research subject but also as an investigative tool. As Denning and Lewis (2016) observe, this represents a fundamental shift toward "data-intensive science," creating hybrid approaches that merge theoretical analysis with empirical validation, particularly evident in large language model research.
The field continues to expand through interdisciplinary integration, with emerging areas like quantum computing and bio-inspired computing bridging computer science with physics and biology. These developments, as suggested by Wing (2006) in her influential work on computational thinking, demonstrate how methodological exchange across disciplines enables solutions to problems previously beyond the reach of any single field.
Addressing big data challenges requires adaptive methodologies incorporating parallel computing and specialized frameworks like MapReduce. Patterson et al. (2012) emphasize that such "scalable architecture" approaches have become essential for managing the unprecedented scale and heterogeneity of contemporary computational problems.
The growing system complexity has elevated the importance of systems science methods, which employ holistic analysis and optimization techniques. As advocated by researchers in distributed systems design, these approaches enable navigation across abstraction levels while maintaining overall system coherence and performance.
Finally, the community has intensified its focus on research reproducibility and quality assurance. Through automated testing, continuous integration, and rigorous validation techniques, computer science is strengthening its scientific foundation—addressing what Hutson (2018) identified as the "replication crisis" in computational research while enhancing methodological credibility.

5 Conclusion: The Multifaceted Integration and Innovative Development of Computer Science Methodology
The methodology of computer science represents a unique fusion of natural science rigor and engineering technology practice, whose core lies in promoting disciplinary development through the cyclical interaction of theoretical exploration, abstract modeling, and system design. This methodological system enables computer science to deeply explore the essential properties of computation while constructing reliable systems to solve practical problems. With the rise of artificial intelligence, big data, and interdisciplinary research, computer science methodology continues to evolve, absorbing the essence of various fields and forming richer and more diverse research paradigms.
For computer science graduate students, mastering this methodological system is crucial. It is not only the foundation for completing academic research but also the key to future success in academia or industry. By understanding and flexibly applying the three paradigms of theory, abstraction, and design, combined with a deep grasp of the 12 fundamental concepts (such as binding, complexity, efficiency, reuse, security, etc.), graduate students can choose appropriate methodological paths for research problems of different natures, maintaining scientific rigor while leveraging technological innovation. This comprehensive and multifaceted methodological literacy is the essential characteristic that distinguishes computer scientists from ordinary programmers and the fundamental guarantee for promoting the continuous progress of computing technology.
